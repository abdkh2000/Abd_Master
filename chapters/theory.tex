\section{Theoretical Background}
\label{sec:theoretical-background}

\subsection{Wall-Bounded Turbulent Channel Flow and Governing Equations}
\label{sec:channel-flow-governing-equations}

Plane channel flow provides a well-defined configuration for studying the
interaction between turbulence and solid walls. It consists of fluid motion
between two stationary, parallel, impermeable walls separated by the full
channel height $2\delta$, where $\delta$ is the channel half-height. The
streamwise, wall-normal, and spanwise coordinates are denoted by $x$, $y$, and
$z$, respectively. The corresponding instantaneous velocity components are
$u$, $v$, and $w$, so that the velocity vector is
$\mathbf{u}=(u,v,w)$. Figure~\ref{fig:channel-geometry} summarizes this
coordinate system. This idealized geometry is widely used for fundamental
studies because it retains the essential influence of no-slip walls while
remaining homogeneous in the two wall-parallel directions
\cite{kimMoinMoser1987}.

The lower and upper walls are located at $y=-\delta$ and $y=\delta$. For
stationary walls, impermeability and the no-slip condition require
\begin{equation}
  \mathbf{u}(x,-\delta,z,t)
  =\mathbf{u}(x,\delta,z,t)=\mathbf{0},
  \label{eq:channel-no-slip}
\end{equation}
where $t$ denotes time. In an ideal channel simulation, the domain is commonly
periodic in $x$ and $z$. If its streamwise and spanwise lengths are $L_x$ and
$L_z$, respectively, the velocity therefore satisfies
\begin{equation}
  \mathbf{u}(x+L_x,y,z,t)=\mathbf{u}(x,y,z,t),
  \qquad
  \mathbf{u}(x,y,z+L_z,t)=\mathbf{u}(x,y,z,t).
  \label{eq:channel-periodicity}
\end{equation}
These periodic conditions represent a spatially repeating section of a channel;
they do not imply that individual turbulent structures are steady.

The fluid is initially treated as Newtonian, incompressible, and of constant
density $\rho$ and constant kinematic viscosity $\nu$. Here, $\rho$ is measured
in $\mathrm{kg\,m^{-3}}$ and $\nu$ in $\mathrm{m^2\,s^{-1}}$. Conservation of
mass then reduces to the incompressible continuity equation
\begin{equation}
  \boldsymbol{\nabla}\mathbin{\cdot}\mathbf{u}=0,
  \label{eq:incompressible-continuity}
\end{equation}
where $\boldsymbol{\nabla}$ is the spatial gradient operator. Conservation of
momentum is governed by the incompressible Navier--Stokes equations
\begin{equation}
  \frac{\partial \mathbf{u}}{\partial t}
  +(\mathbf{u}\mathbin{\cdot}\boldsymbol{\nabla})\mathbf{u}
  =-\frac{1}{\rho}\boldsymbol{\nabla}p
  +\nu\boldsymbol{\nabla}^{2}\mathbf{u},
  \label{eq:incompressible-navier-stokes}
\end{equation}
in which $p$ is the pressure in pascals and
$\boldsymbol{\nabla}^{2}$ is the Laplace operator. The terms in
Eq.~\eqref{eq:incompressible-navier-stokes} describe, from left to right,
local acceleration, convective acceleration, acceleration caused by the
pressure gradient, and viscous momentum diffusion. Equations
\eqref{eq:incompressible-continuity} and
\eqref{eq:incompressible-navier-stokes}, together with the boundary conditions
in Eqs.~\eqref{eq:channel-no-slip} and \eqref{eq:channel-periodicity}, form the
dimensional mechanical description used here \cite{pope2000}.

A streamwise pressure gradient is required to maintain a non-zero flow through
the channel. A convenient representation separates the pressure into a linear
mean contribution and a periodic remainder $\widetilde{p}$:
\begin{equation}
  p(x,y,z,t)=-Gx+\widetilde{p}(x,y,z,t),
  \qquad
  G=-\frac{\mathrm{d}\langle p\rangle}{\mathrm{d}x}>0,
  \label{eq:channel-pressure-decomposition}
\end{equation}
where $G$ is the imposed streamwise pressure drop per unit length, measured in
$\mathrm{Pa\,m^{-1}}$, and angle brackets denote a statistical average. The
periodic pressure remainder $\widetilde{p}$ can satisfy the same streamwise
periodicity as the velocity. Substitution of
Eq.~\eqref{eq:channel-pressure-decomposition} into
Eq.~\eqref{eq:incompressible-navier-stokes} gives
\begin{equation}
  \frac{\partial \mathbf{u}}{\partial t}
  +(\mathbf{u}\mathbin{\cdot}\boldsymbol{\nabla})\mathbf{u}
  =-\frac{1}{\rho}\boldsymbol{\nabla}\widetilde{p}
  +\nu\boldsymbol{\nabla}^{2}\mathbf{u}
  +\frac{G}{\rho}\mathbf{e}_x,
  \label{eq:pressure-driven-navier-stokes}
\end{equation}
where $\mathbf{e}_x$ is the unit vector in the positive streamwise direction.
Thus, the mean pressure gradient appears as a uniform streamwise acceleration.
Numerically, an equivalent forcing can be adjusted in time to maintain a
prescribed flow rate rather than a prescribed value of $G$.

Fully developed flow is understood in a statistical sense. Its averaged
velocity and turbulence statistics are independent of $x$ and $z$, although
the instantaneous turbulent field remains three-dimensional and time-dependent.
For the velocity, this condition is expressed as
\begin{equation}
  \langle\mathbf{u}\rangle=\bigl(U(y),0,0\bigr),
  \label{eq:fully-developed-channel-flow}
\end{equation}
where $U(y)=\langle u\rangle$ is the mean streamwise velocity. The averaging
operation and the turbulent fluctuations about this mean will be formalized in
the subsequent treatment of turbulence statistics.

For a bulk scaling, the bulk mean velocity is defined by
\begin{equation}
  U_b=\frac{1}{2\delta}\int_{-\delta}^{\delta}U(y)\,\mathrm{d}y.
  \label{eq:channel-bulk-velocity}
\end{equation}
Using $\delta$ as the reference length and $U_b$ as the reference velocity
introduces the dimensionless variables
\begin{equation}
  \mathbf{x}^{*}=\frac{\mathbf{x}}{\delta},
  \qquad
  t^{*}=\frac{tU_b}{\delta},
  \qquad
  \mathbf{u}^{*}=\frac{\mathbf{u}}{U_b},
  \qquad
  p^{*}=\frac{\widetilde{p}}{\rho U_b^2},
  \label{eq:channel-bulk-scaling}
\end{equation}
where a superscript $*$ identifies a dimensionless quantity. Equations
\eqref{eq:incompressible-continuity} and
\eqref{eq:pressure-driven-navier-stokes} then become
\begin{align}
  \boldsymbol{\nabla}^{*}\mathbin{\cdot}\mathbf{u}^{*}&=0,
  \label{eq:dimensionless-continuity}\\
  \frac{\partial\mathbf{u}^{*}}{\partial t^{*}}
  +(\mathbf{u}^{*}\mathbin{\cdot}\boldsymbol{\nabla}^{*})\mathbf{u}^{*}
  &=-\boldsymbol{\nabla}^{*}p^{*}
  +\frac{1}{\mathrm{Re}_b}\boldsymbol{\nabla}^{*2}\mathbf{u}^{*}
  +\Pi\mathbf{e}_x,
  \label{eq:dimensionless-channel-momentum}
\end{align}
with
\begin{equation}
  \mathrm{Re}_b=\frac{U_b\delta}{\nu},
  \qquad
  \Pi=\frac{G\delta}{\rho U_b^2}.
  \label{eq:bulk-reynolds-pressure-parameter}
\end{equation}
Here, $\boldsymbol{\nabla}^{*}$ denotes differentiation with respect to
$\mathbf{x}^{*}$, $\mathrm{Re}_b$ is the bulk Reynolds number based on the
channel half-height, and $\Pi$ is the dimensionless pressure-gradient parameter.
Some literature defines the bulk Reynolds number using the full height
$2\delta$; stating the reference length is therefore necessary when values are
compared. This bulk scaling exposes the relative strength of inertia and
viscosity. A later subsection will introduce the complementary friction-velocity
scaling needed to describe the near-wall region.
