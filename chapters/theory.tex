\section{Theoretical Background}
\label{sec:theoretical-background}

This chapter introduces only the theory required to formulate the planned
channel-flow simulations and assess their statistics. Common coordinate and
scaling conventions provide a consistent basis for the unstratified,
passive-scalar, and stably stratified cases.

\subsection{Physical Configuration and Governing Equations}
\label{sec:physical-configuration-governing-equations}

The configuration is plane pressure-driven flow between two stationary,
parallel walls. The streamwise, spanwise, and wall-normal coordinates are
$x$, $y$, and $z$, and the corresponding velocity components are $u$, $v$,
and $w$. The velocity vector is $\mathbf{u}=(u,v,w)$. The walls are at
$z=\pm h$, where the channel half-height is $h=L_z/2$; the periodic domain
lengths are $L_x$ and $L_y$ in the wall-parallel directions.

For a Newtonian fluid with constant reference density $\rho_0$ and kinematic
viscosity $\nu$, the unstratified baseline satisfies the incompressible,
pressure-driven Navier--Stokes equations. Writing the pressure as a linear
mean part and a periodic remainder, $p=-Gx+\widetilde p$, with
$G=-\mathrm{d}\langle p\rangle/\mathrm{d}x>0$, gives
\begin{equation}
  \begin{aligned}
    \boldsymbol{\nabla}\mathbin{\cdot}\mathbf{u}&=0,\\
    \frac{\partial\mathbf{u}}{\partial t}
      +(\mathbf{u}\mathbin{\cdot}\boldsymbol{\nabla})\mathbf{u}
      &=-\frac{1}{\rho_0}\boldsymbol{\nabla}\widetilde p
        +\nu\boldsymbol{\nabla}^2\mathbf{u}
        +\frac{G}{\rho_0}\mathbf{e}_x .
  \end{aligned}
  \label{eq:baseline-governing-equations}
\end{equation}
Here $\mathbf{e}_x$ is the streamwise unit vector. The uniform acceleration
$G\mathbf{e}_x/\rho_0$ represents the imposed mean pressure gradient; an
equivalent constant-flow-rate formulation would adjust this forcing in time
but would not change the statistical definitions below \cite{pope2000}.

No slip and no penetration apply at both walls, while velocity is periodic in
$x$ and $y$. These conditions are
\begin{equation}
  \begin{aligned}
    \mathbf{u}(x,y,\pm h,t)&=\mathbf{0},\\
    \mathbf{u}(x+L_x,y,z,t)&=\mathbf{u}(x,y,z,t),\\
    \mathbf{u}(x,y+L_y,z,t)&=\mathbf{u}(x,y,z,t).
  \end{aligned}
  \label{eq:channel-boundary-conditions}
\end{equation}
The periodic pressure remainder and transported scalar obey the corresponding
wall-parallel periodicity. Statistically fully developed channel flow means
that, after transients, its statistics are stationary and independent of the
homogeneous coordinates $x$ and $y$. The mean velocity consequently has the
form $\langle\mathbf{u}\rangle=(U(z),0,0)$ even though the instantaneous
turbulent field remains three-dimensional and unsteady
\cite{kimMoinMoser1987}.

\subsection{Turbulence Statistics and Wall Scaling}
\label{sec:turbulence-statistics-wall-scaling}

Channel statistics combine an average over the two homogeneous planes with a
time average over a statistically stationary production interval. For any
instantaneous quantity $\phi$, an interval of duration $\mathcal{T}$ beginning
at $t_0$ defines the finite-data average adopted below. The Reynolds
decomposition is then
\begin{equation}
  \begin{aligned}
    \langle\phi\rangle_{xy}(z,t)
      &=\frac{1}{L_xL_y}\int_0^{L_y}\!\int_0^{L_x}
        \phi(x,y,z,t)\,\mathrm{d}x\,\mathrm{d}y,\\
    \langle\phi\rangle(z)
      &=\frac{1}{\mathcal{T}}\int_{t_0}^{t_0+\mathcal{T}}
        \langle\phi\rangle_{xy}(z,t)\,\mathrm{d}t,\\
    u_i&=U_i+u_i',
    &U_i&=\langle u_i\rangle,
    \qquad i\in\{1,2,3\},\\
    \langle u_i'\rangle&=0.
  \end{aligned}
  \label{eq:channel-average-reynolds-decomposition}
\end{equation}
The project assignments are $u_1=u$, $u_2=v$, and $u_3=w$, while
$U(z)=\langle u\rangle$ is the mean streamwise velocity. In the infinite-time
limit the average is independent of $t_0$; in a calculation, stationarity and
convergence checks must support that independence.

The velocity quantities required for later comparison are the three
root-mean-square (RMS) fluctuations, the Reynolds shear stress, and turbulent
kinetic energy per unit mass:
\begin{equation}
  \begin{aligned}
    u_{\mathrm{rms}}&=\sqrt{\langle u'^2\rangle},
    &v_{\mathrm{rms}}&=\sqrt{\langle v'^2\rangle},
    &w_{\mathrm{rms}}&=\sqrt{\langle w'^2\rangle},\\
    \tau_{uw}^{\mathrm{turb}}&=-\rho_0\langle u'w'\rangle,
    &k&=\frac{1}{2}\left(
      \langle u'^2\rangle+\langle v'^2\rangle+\langle w'^2\rangle
      \right).
  \end{aligned}
  \label{eq:velocity-validation-statistics}
\end{equation}
The correlation $-\langle u'w'\rangle$ is the kinematic turbulent transport
of streamwise momentum in the wall-normal direction. Together, the RMS
profiles and $k$ show the magnitude and anisotropy of the velocity
fluctuations \cite{pope2000,kimMoinMoser1987}.

BRINE uses $z$ and $w$ for the wall-normal coordinate and velocity. Channel
data that instead use the common literature convention $(y,v)$ in the
wall-normal direction must therefore be remapped as
$y_{\mathrm{lit}}\mapsto z$ and $v_{\mathrm{lit}}\mapsto w$; in particular,
$-\langle u'v'\rangle_{\mathrm{lit}}$ corresponds to
$-\langle u'w'\rangle$ here. The mapping must be stated explicitly in each
external comparison.

The positive wall-shear magnitude, friction velocity, and friction Reynolds
number provide the natural channel scales:
\begin{equation}
  \begin{aligned}
    \tau_{w,\pm}&=\rho_0\nu
      \left|\frac{\mathrm{d}U}{\mathrm{d}z}\right|_{z=\pm h},
    &\tau_w&=\frac{\tau_{w,-}+\tau_{w,+}}{2}=hG,\\
    u_\tau&=\sqrt{\frac{\tau_w}{\rho_0}},
    &Re_\tau&=\frac{u_\tau h}{\nu}.
  \end{aligned}
  \label{eq:wall-friction-scales}
\end{equation}
For the reflection-symmetric baseline,
$\tau_{w,-}=\tau_{w,+}=\tau_w$. The averaged equality $\tau_w=hG$ follows from
the fully developed mean momentum balance and also supplies a forcing-based
case scale if thermal conditions make the individual wall stresses unequal.
Any local wall scaling based instead on $u_{\tau,\pm}$ must be identified in
the comparison.

Let $d_w=h-|z|$ be distance from the nearest wall. The viscous length and the
inner-scaled quantities used for profile comparisons are
\begin{equation}
  \begin{aligned}
    \ell_\nu&=\frac{\nu}{u_\tau},
    &z^+&=\frac{d_w}{\ell_\nu}=\frac{d_wu_\tau}{\nu},
    &U^+&=\frac{U}{u_\tau},\\
    (u_{\mathrm{rms}}^+,v_{\mathrm{rms}}^+,w_{\mathrm{rms}}^+)
      &=\frac{1}{u_\tau}
        (u_{\mathrm{rms}},v_{\mathrm{rms}},w_{\mathrm{rms}}),
    &-\langle u'w'\rangle^+
      &=-\frac{\langle u'w'\rangle}{u_\tau^2},
    &k^+&=\frac{k}{u_\tau^2}.
  \end{aligned}
  \label{eq:inner-scaled-statistics}
\end{equation}
Thus $z^+$ denotes a non-negative wall distance rather than the signed physical
coordinate.

For a smooth wall, the leading viscous behaviour and the overlap approximation
for the mean velocity can be stated compactly as
\begin{equation}
  U^+=z^+\quad\text{(viscous limit)},
  \qquad
  U^+=\frac{1}{\kappa_{\mathrm{vK}}}\ln z^+ + B
  \quad\text{(logarithmic overlap)}.
  \label{eq:mean-velocity-wall-laws}
\end{equation}
Here $\kappa_{\mathrm{vK}}$ and $B$ are the von K\'arm\'an constant and
intercept, not fitted project parameters. These relations are reference
behaviours rather than a complete division of the channel into textbook
regions. The planned $Re_\tau=590$ baseline will be assessed through the
profiles defined above and comparison with established channel DNS
\cite{moserKimMansour1999}.

\subsection{Scalar Transport and Thermal Buoyancy}
\label{sec:scalar-transport-thermal-buoyancy}

Let $T(\mathbf{x},t)$ denote dimensional temperature, with molecular thermal
diffusivity $\kappa$. Its constant-property advection--diffusion equation and
the Prandtl number are
\begin{equation}
  \begin{aligned}
    \frac{\partial T}{\partial t}
      +\mathbf{u}\mathbin{\cdot}\boldsymbol{\nabla}T
      &=\kappa\boldsymbol{\nabla}^2T+Q,
    &Pr&=\frac{\nu}{\kappa}.
  \end{aligned}
  \label{eq:temperature-transport-prandtl}
\end{equation}
The source $Q$ has units of temperature per unit time and is retained because
the planned passive-scalar benchmark may use uniform internal heating; it is
zero when no volumetric source is present. A nondimensional solver variable
$\theta=(T-T_0)/\Delta T$, where $\Delta T>0$ is its chosen temperature
scale, obeys an equation of the same form only after its source and boundary
values have been scaled consistently. Dimensional $T$ is used below wherever
density or buoyancy is defined.

Applying the average in Eq.~\eqref{eq:channel-average-reynolds-decomposition}
gives the mean temperature $\Theta(z)=\langle T\rangle$ and fluctuation
$T'=T-\Theta$. The principal thermal statistics are
\begin{equation}
  \begin{aligned}
    T_{\mathrm{rms}}(z)&=\sqrt{\langle T'^2\rangle},
    &F_{T,z}(z)&=\langle w'T'\rangle.
  \end{aligned}
  \label{eq:temperature-statistics}
\end{equation}
The wall-normal turbulent temperature flux $F_{T,z}$ measures transport by
velocity fluctuations and is distinct from molecular diffusion. The same
definitions apply to a nondimensional passive scalar after replacing
$(T,\Theta,T')$ by its consistently scaled variables.

When a wall-scaled mean temperature is reported, define the outward-normal
kinematic wall flux $j_w$, friction temperature $T_\tau$, and plotted mean by
\begin{equation}
  j_w=-\kappa\left.\frac{\partial\Theta}{\partial n}\right|_w,
  \qquad
  T_\tau=\frac{|j_w|}{u_\tau},
  \qquad
  \Theta^+=s_w\frac{\Theta_w-\Theta}{T_\tau}.
  \label{eq:temperature-wall-scaling}
\end{equation}
Here $n$ is the outward wall normal, $\Theta_w$ is the wall temperature, and
$s_w=\pm1$ states the orientation used for each plotted wall. These quantities
are evaluated separately at each wall and only when $j_w\neq0$; a zero-flux
wall has no finite $T_\tau$ scaling. The explicit factor avoids silently
imposing a temperature sign convention, and $z^+$ remains the wall-distance
coordinate.

Temperature is passive when Eq.~\eqref{eq:temperature-transport-prandtl} is
solved without feedback to momentum. It is active when density changes create
a buoyancy force, so that velocity transports temperature and temperature in
turn modifies velocity. For small relative density variations, the Boussinesq
approximation uses a reference density $\rho_0$, temperature $T_0$, and thermal
expansion coefficient $\alpha_T$. With the physical gravity vector
$\mathbf{g}$ pointing in the direction in which gravity acts, let
$\widetilde p_b$ denote the periodic dynamic-pressure remainder after removal
of the reference hydrostatic pressure. The density law and active momentum
equation are
\begin{equation}
  \begin{aligned}
    \rho(T)&=\rho_0\left[1-\alpha_T(T-T_0)\right],\\
    \frac{\partial\mathbf{u}}{\partial t}
      +(\mathbf{u}\mathbin{\cdot}\boldsymbol{\nabla})\mathbf{u}
      &=-\frac{1}{\rho_0}\boldsymbol{\nabla}\widetilde p_b
        +\nu\boldsymbol{\nabla}^2\mathbf{u}
        +\frac{G}{\rho_0}\mathbf{e}_x
        -\alpha_T(T-T_0)\mathbf{g}.
  \end{aligned}
  \label{eq:boussinesq-density-momentum}
\end{equation}
The last term is the thermal buoyancy acceleration. For $\alpha_T>0$, a warm
perturbation accelerates opposite to $\mathbf{g}$ under this convention. The
exact wall values, gravity orientation, temperature
normalisation, buoyancy-variable spelling, coefficient mapping, and input sign
used by BRINE must be fixed from the verified case input rather than inferred
from Eq.~\eqref{eq:boussinesq-density-momentum} \cite{pope2000}.

\subsection{Stable Stratification and Internal-Wave Relevance}
\label{sec:stable-stratification-wave-relevance}

Stable stratification places denser fluid below lighter fluid in the
gravitational sense, so that a displaced parcel experiences a restoring
buoyancy force. Let $s$ increase opposite to $\mathbf{g}$ and let
$g=|\mathbf{g}|$. For the dimensional Boussinesq temperature, stability means
that the mean density decreases with $s$, or equivalently that the mean
temperature increases with $s$ for $\alpha_T>0$.

For the project-relevant configuration in which gravity is wall-normal, so
$s$ is aligned with $z$ up to sign, the buoyancy frequency, local gradient
Richardson number, and case-level friction Richardson number quantify
complementary aspects of this state:
\begin{equation}
  \begin{aligned}
    N^2(s)&=-\frac{g}{\rho_0}
      \frac{\mathrm{d}\langle\rho\rangle}{\mathrm{d}s}
      =g\alpha_T\frac{\mathrm{d}\Theta}{\mathrm{d}s},\\
    Ri_g(s)&=\frac{N^2(s)}{
      \left(\mathrm{d}U/\mathrm{d}s\right)^2},\\
    Ri_\tau&=\frac{g h\,\Delta\rho_\star}{\rho_0u_\tau^2}
      =\frac{b_\star h}{u_\tau^2},
    &b_\star&=g\frac{\Delta\rho_\star}{\rho_0}.
  \end{aligned}
  \label{eq:stratification-scales}
\end{equation}
Here $N^2>0$ denotes stable stratification. The quantity $Ri_g$ compares local
buoyancy and mean shear where the shear is non-zero; it is not a universal
transition criterion and can be ill-conditioned near a zero-shear centreline.
The positive contrast $\Delta\rho_\star$ is the imposed stable wall-to-wall
density difference, so $Ri_\tau$ compares buoyancy over $h$ with wall-friction
inertia. For dimensional temperature,
$\Delta\rho_\star/\rho_0\simeq\alpha_T\Delta T_\star$, where
$\Delta T_\star>0$ is the temperature at larger $s$ minus that at smaller $s$.
Mapping this definition to a nondimensional case parameter requires the
verified temperature scale
\cite{garciaVillalbaDelAlamo2011}.

Stable buoyancy opposes wall-normal displacement and therefore competes with
shear production. Consequences relevant here include weaker wall-normal
momentum and scalar transport, modification or suppression of core turbulence,
and increased anisotropy. The response need not be uniform across the channel:
near-wall shear can sustain turbulence while buoyancy strongly affects the
more weakly sheared core
\cite{armenioSarkar2002,garciaVillalbaDelAlamo2011}.

Internal gravity waves are possible because stable buoyancy supplies a
restoring force. In the ideal inviscid, uniformly stratified limit, a
propagating wave has intrinsic angular frequency
$0<|\widehat{\omega}|<N$, where $N=\sqrt{N^2}$; mean shear, confinement,
viscosity, and turbulence
modify that simple picture. Temperature or velocity patterns that appear
wave-like are therefore only candidates. An internal-wave interpretation
requires coherent evidence from the simulation data that is consistent with
the buoyancy time scale and spatial structure, not an isolated field snapshot
or oscillation \cite{armenioSarkar2002}.

\subsection{DNS Requirements and Application to the Project}
\label{sec:dns-requirements-project-application}

Direct numerical simulation (DNS) advances the governing velocity and scalar
equations without a turbulence-closure model. Calling a calculation DNS also
requires evidence that the dynamically relevant velocity and thermal scales
are resolved. Mesh resolution, time resolution, domain size, and statistical
convergence must therefore be demonstrated for the quantities used in the
project comparisons \cite{pope2000}.

For a channel grid, nominal spacings may be reported in the same inner units as
the turbulence statistics:
\begin{equation}
  \Delta x^+=\frac{\Delta x\,u_\tau}{\nu},
  \qquad
  \Delta y^+=\frac{\Delta y\,u_\tau}{\nu},
  \qquad
  \Delta z_j^+=\frac{\Delta z_j\,u_\tau}{\nu}.
  \label{eq:dns-grid-wall-units}
\end{equation}
The local $\Delta z_j^+$ allows for wall-normal stretching. These measures do
not by themselves prove adequate resolution: the numerical method, the
resolved profiles, and sensitivity to refinement also matter. The domain must
contain the important large structures, and the time step and stored data must
resolve the dynamics being interpreted. Actual point counts, stretching,
domain lengths, time step, stability limits, and output intervals belong to
the numerical methodology and results.

Production averages begin only after initial transients decay and monitored
quantities support statistical stationarity. Within stated uncertainty, the
averages should be insensitive to reasonable changes in the production window.
Grid and time-step sensitivity address discretisation error, while comparison
with reference DNS assesses the combined calculation under compatible
conditions; none substitutes for the others. These checks apply to velocity
statistics and, when relevant, to $\Theta$, $T_{\mathrm{rms}}$, and
$\langle w'T'\rangle$.

The planned sequence applies these definitions progressively. An unstratified
channel at $Re_\tau=590$ first establishes the workflow and permits comparison
of $U^+$, the RMS fluctuations, Reynolds shear stress, and $k$ with trusted
DNS. A passive-scalar channel at $Pr=1$ then tests $\Theta$,
$T_{\mathrm{rms}}$, and $\langle w'T'\rangle$ with buoyancy disabled; its exact
run configuration belongs to the methodology chapter. Finally, the active
stable target at $Re_\tau=180$, $Pr=0.7$, and $Ri_\tau=120$ addresses changes
in mixing, anisotropy, core turbulence, and possible wave-like motion. These
parameters are planned targets, not simulation results.

Adequate resolution and converged statistics are prerequisites throughout.
Isolating buoyancy effects in the stable case additionally requires a
compatible unstratified reference or an explicit treatment of differences in
$Re_\tau$ and other operating conditions.
